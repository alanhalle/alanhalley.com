My great-great-great-great-grandfather's travels

Three wagons, thirteen people, the Bee Trace, and a broken axle.

Family History · Missouri · About 190 Years Ago
Back to writing

Henry Halley was born in Fairfax County, Virginia, around 1786. He moved to Kentucky as a young man — most likely through the Cumberland Gap on the Wilderness Road, the same route Daniel Boone had cut through the Appalachians a generation before. He settled in Clark County and Bourbon County, in the Bluegrass region, and stayed for twenty-six years. Then he moved again.

He had fought in the War of 1812. He was in his late forties when he decided to leave Kentucky. His wife Polly was the same age. They had seven children still living, the oldest twenty-six and the youngest eight.

They almost certainly came by water for at least part of the journey. By 1833 there was regular flatboat and steamboat traffic on the Ohio River and the Mississippi. The practical route from central Kentucky to Missouri went down the Ohio to its confluence with the Mississippi at Cairo, Illinois, then north on the Mississippi to the Missouri River, then upriver into Missouri. Three rivers, ferries at crossings, the broad flat current of the Ohio carrying them west.

From the Missouri River they turned north. The route ran along the Bee Trace — an ancient path through the high ground between Missouri's river drainages, used first by the Sac, Fox, and Iowa nations, then by the first wave of settlers from Kentucky and Virginia. The trace followed ridgelines. Wagons on ridgelines avoided the creek crossings and the bottomland mud that could stop a team for days.

Henry was driving at least one wagon. His oldest son James — twenty-six, married the year before, his wife pregnant — almost certainly had his own. So did his second daughter Elizabeth, traveling with her husband and an infant born that June. Three wagons, thirteen or fourteen people, heading north from the Missouri River into unorganized territory.

The county they were heading for didn't exist yet. Macon County wasn't organized until January 1837.

The axle broke in Woodville. It was eight miles east of where they would eventually settle. They stayed.

Henry's wife was Mary "Polly" Patton Halley. The family pronounced the name Holly. She was Polly Holly — the rhyme only works in the old pronunciation. She probably heard her own name as a rhyme her whole life.

Henry and Polly are buried in a private plot in Macon County. Two graves, nobody else. Five generations of their descendants are buried at Mount Salem Cemetery in Excello, four miles from where the axle broke, the oldest since 1881 and the most recent in 2015. A hundred and thirty-four years.

The wagon broke and they stayed. Then they stayed. Then they stayed. Six generations in the same county.

There are other stories in this thread — about the name, which started as Hawley in colonial Virginia and drifted twice before arriving at the pronunciation my father's generation settled on. About the land itself, which became a strip mine a century after Henry farmed it, and three generations of his descendants who worked it for wages. About a Christmas visit in 1995, and what an old man said over lunch about a broken axle, and what I found when I drove to Woodville afterward.

Those are for another time.
